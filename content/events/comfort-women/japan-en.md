The Japanese government has expressed sincere apologies and remorse for the so-called "comfort women" issue, stating that it deeply damaged the honor and dignity of many women (1993 Kono Statement).

The description of the comfort women issue in Japanese textbooks has changed over time. In the 1990s, multiple textbooks described them in relatively detail as "comfort women," but since the 2000s, the description has continued to be reduced or deleted. Although the term "comfort women" has started to appear again in textbooks in recent years, the descriptions are generally concise and often limit themselves to expressions such as "women were gathered from the Korean Peninsula, China, and elsewhere in comfort facilities set up on war zones."

The Japanese government takes the position that the comfort women issue between Japan and South Korea has been legally resolved by the Japan-Korea Claims Agreement of 1965. Additionally, in 1995, the Asian Women's Fund was established to carry out a project to deliver ``atonement money'' and a letter of apology from the Prime Minister to former comfort women. In the 2015 Japan-South Korea agreement, the Japanese government contributed 1 billion yen to a foundation established by the South Korean government, confirming a "final and irreversible solution" to the issue. Although the Japanese government claims that no documents have been found that directly demonstrate forced abduction by the military or authorities, it acknowledges that life in the comfort stations was harrowing under coercive conditions.
