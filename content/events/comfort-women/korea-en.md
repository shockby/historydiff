The Japanese military "comfort women" system is a system of wartime sexual violence in which women from all over Asia, including the Korean peninsula, were systematically mobilized by the Japanese military and forced to live as sexual slaves during the colonial rule and war of aggression by Japanese imperialism.

In South Korean textbooks, the "comfort women" issue is described in detail as one of the most serious human rights violations under colonial rule. Many young women were taken away under the guise of volunteer corps or through sweet talk, fraud, and violence, and were forced to perform sexual services to Japanese soldiers while deprived of their freedom at battlefield comfort stations. The number of victims is estimated to range from **tens of thousands to more than 200,000**.

The Korean government and civil society have consistently called for the Japanese government to acknowledge its legal responsibility, issue an official apology, and provide compensation. After Kim Hak-sun testified under her real name for the first time in 1991, Wednesday demonstrations were held every Wednesday in front of the Japanese Embassy in Seoul since 1992, and in 2011, the Statue of the Girl of Peace, symbolizing the comfort women, was erected. The South Korean side maintains that the 1965 Japan-Korea Claims Agreement concerns economic cooperation between nations, and that the right of individuals to seek reparations for anti-humanitarian torts such as the comfort women issue has not been extinguished. The 2015 Japan-Korea agreement was also effectively reviewed under the Moon Jae-in administration, as it did not reflect the wishes of the victims. In 2021, the Seoul Central District Court ordered the Japanese government to pay compensation of 100 million won to each victim.
