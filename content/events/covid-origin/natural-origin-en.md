The theory is that SARS-CoV-2, like many other coronaviruses, is a zoonotic disease that spread naturally from animals to humans. A mechanism similar to that of SARS (2003, via palm civet) and MERS (2012, via dromedary camel) is assumed.

Bats are the largest reservoir (host) of coronaviruses in nature, and the RaTG13 virus discovered in China's Yunnan province has approximately 96% genomic sequence identity with SARS-CoV-2. However, this 4% difference represents an evolutionary gap of several decades, suggesting that RaTG13 is not a direct ancestral virus. The virus is thought to have infected humans via some intermediate host.

A study published in *Science* and *Cell* in 2022 reported analysis that strongly indicates that the Wuhan Huanan Seafood Market was the epicenter of the initial outbreak. Viral RNA was detected in environmental samples from the market, showing that it was particularly concentrated in areas where live wild animals were sold, and that two independent strains of the virus were associated with the market.

The 2021 WHO-China joint investigation team evaluated the natural origin theory as "likely to very likely," but a definitive intermediate host animal has not been identified as of 2026. Pangolins, raccoon dogs, and mink are being investigated as possible candidates.
