In 1966, Mao Zedong misjudged the situation in the process of socialist construction and launched the "Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution" under the pretext of "preventing the revival of capitalism." This was a civil war that was mistakenly initiated by the leaders and exploited by counter-revolutionary groups (Lin Biao and Jiang Qing counter-revolutionary groups), which brought serious disaster to the Party, the state, and the people as a whole.

The Red Guard movement spread throughout the country, calling for the destruction of "old ideas, old culture, old customs, and old customs" (the Four Olds). Many cadres and intellectuals were persecuted, and Party leaders, including President Liu Shaoqi, were unfairly criticized and overthrown. Educational institutions were closed and the national economy suffered a severe blow.

The Cultural Revolution ended in 1976 with the death of Mao Zedong and the arrest of the Gang of Four. In 1981, in the ``Resolution on some historical issues of the Party since the founding of the People's Republic,'' the Cultural Revolution was clearly rejected, stating that it was ``not a revolution or social progress in any sense.'' Based on this historical lesson, the Chinese Communist Party decided to switch to a reform and opening-up policy and promote socialist modernization construction.
