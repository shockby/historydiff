In 1966, Mao Zedong launched the "Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution" in the People's Republic of China. The movement was largely a political struggle for Mao Zedong, whose influence within the party had declined due to the failure of the Great Leap Forward, to overthrow Liu Shaoqi and Deng Xiaoping's pragmatic factions (actual power factions) and regain power.

Mao Zedong mobilized students and young men called the Red Guards to attack party officials, intellectuals, teachers, and others as "money-movers" (those who followed the path of capitalism) under the slogan "There is reason to rebel." Destruction of traditional culture and cultural assets (the ``hashikushi'') took place in various places, and society fell into extreme chaos. Schools were closed for long periods and many young people were "released" to rural areas.

This turmoil continued for about 10 years, ending in 1976 with the death of Mao Zedong and the arrest of the Gang of Four. The Cultural Revolution left serious scars on Chinese society, inflicting enormous damage on all areas of economy, education, and culture. Subsequently, under Deng Xiaoping, reform and opening-up policies were promoted, and China made a major shift toward the introduction of a market economy.
