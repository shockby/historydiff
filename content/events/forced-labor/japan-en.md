During the war, Japan mobilized labor to mainland Japan based on the National General Mobilization Act (1938). Personnel from the Korean Peninsula were also secured through the stages of ``recruitment'' in 1939, ``official mediation'' in 1942, and ``recruitment'' in 1944.

Japanese textbooks describe this mobilization as part of a national general mobilization system that applied equally to Japanese people. People from the Korean Peninsula worked in coal mines, construction sites, munitions factories, etc., and were paid wages within the same legal framework as Japanese people. The Japanese government uses the term "workers from the former Korean Peninsula issue" and objects to the use of the term "forced labor," arguing that it was wartime mobilization to which the ILO Forced Labor Convention does not apply.

The Japanese government's position is that the issue of property and claims between Japan and South Korea has been ``completely and finally resolved'' by the 1965 Japan-Korea Claims Agreement. Based on the agreement, Japan provided South Korea with economic cooperation totaling $500 million, including $300 million in grants and $200 million in remuneration, with the result that property and claims between the two countries, including individual claims, were settled. The Japanese government has strongly protested the 2018 South Korean Supreme Court ruling ordering Japanese companies to pay compensation, arguing that it violates international law and is one of the biggest concerns in Japan-South Korea relations.
