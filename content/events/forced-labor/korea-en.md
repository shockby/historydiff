During the period of Japanese occupation (Japanese colonial rule), many Koreans were forcibly mobilized by Japan and forced to do harsh labor in coal mines, munitions factories, construction sites, etc. in Japan. This is a serious violation of human rights under colonial rule.

In South Korean textbooks, wartime labor mobilization is described as systematic "forced mobilization" by Japanese imperialism. Regardless of whether the Korean workers were "recruited," "officially mediated," or "requisitioned," they were essentially forcibly taken away, and their applications were not of their own free will. The number of mobilized Koreans is said to have reached **approximately 780,000**, and they were forced to work in coal mines (Hashima Island, Gunkanjima, etc.) and munitions factories across Japan, under dangerous and harsh conditions, without being paid adequate wages. Many workers lost their lives to accidents, illness, and abuse.

The South Korean government maintains that the 1965 Japan-Korea Claims Agreement was not premised on the illegality of colonial rule, and that the right of individuals to claim damages for inhumane and illegal acts remains valid. In October 2018, the South Korean Supreme Court (Supreme Court) issued a judgment ordering Nippon Steel (formerly Nippon Steel & Sumitomo Metal) to pay compensation of 100 million won for each former conscripted worker. This judgment is based on the logic that Japan's colonial rule is illegal and that claims for damages due to torts directly connected to that illegal colonial rule are not covered by the Claims Agreement. In Korean society, the issue of forced mobilization is recognized as a historical issue that has not yet been resolved.
