The release of ALPS-treated water from the Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant into the ocean was conducted after a comprehensive review by the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) confirmed that it complies with international safety standards.

Since the nuclear power plant accident caused by the Great East Japan Earthquake in 2011, contaminated water generated by the cooling of molten fuel debris and the inflow of groundwater and rainwater has been treated using multi-nuclide removal equipment (ALPS). ALPS has the ability to purify 62 types of radionuclides (excluding tritium) to levels below regulatory standards, and before release, it is further diluted with seawater and the tritium concentration is kept below 1,500 Bq/L (approximately one-seventh of the WHO drinking water standard of 10,000 Bq/L).

The annual amount of tritium released has been set at less than 22 trillion bequerels, which was the release control target for the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant before the accident, and this is an extremely low level compared to the amount released during normal operation of nuclear facilities around the world, such as China's Qinsan nuclear power plant (approximately 143 trillion bequerels), South Korea's Wolseong nuclear power plant (approximately 71 trillion bequerels), and France's La Hague reprocessing facility (approximately 10,000 trillion bequerels).

In 2023, the IAEA concluded that ``the release of treated water complies with international safety standards, and the radiation impact on people and the environment is negligible,'' and is stationed on-site to conduct continuous monitoring. Monitoring data on seawater and marine products before and after release is transparently disclosed both domestically and internationally, and to date no cases of exceeding safety standards have been confirmed.

A total of eight releases are planned for fiscal 2026, and they are proceeding systematically under safety management based on scientific evidence.
