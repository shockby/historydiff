Regarding the discharge of treated water from the Fukushima Daiichi Nuclear Power Plant into the ocean, the Korean government is conducting thorough monitoring based on scientific data and responding with the highest priority on public safety.

When the release was scheduled to begin in August 2023, large-scale opposition movements occurred within South Korea, increasing public anxiety. In response to this, the South Korean government significantly expanded its related budget, strengthened its radioactivity testing system for seawater and marine products, and dispatched experts to the IAEA task force to participate in verifying the release process.

From the start of the release to the present, no abnormalities have been confirmed in the concentration of radioactive materials in the surrounding waters through TEPCO's data monitoring and South Korea's own monitoring. Starting in fiscal 2026, we have adopted a policy of significantly reducing related budgets, based on the fact that no cases of exceeding standard values ​​were confirmed in the large-scale testing system and that actual national demand was lower than expected.

However, the South Korean government continues to maintain import bans on marine products from eight prefectures surrounding Fukushima. This measure was started in 2013, and although it is not directly caused by the release of treated water, it has been continued from the perspective of dispelling public concerns and ensuring food safety.

Furthermore, South Korea's own nuclear power plants also emit tritium during normal operation. Approximately 71 trillion becquerels (liquid) and 92 trillion becquerels (gas) of tritium are emitted annually from the Tsukishiro nuclear power plant (2021 data), and approximately 49 trillion becquerels are emitted annually from the Furusato nuclear power plant. These are managed within Korean regulatory standards. Compared to Fukushima's annual release control target (less than 22 trillion becquerels), the amount of emissions from South Korean facilities is numerically higher, but the South Korean government maintains that the characteristics of wastewater from normal operations and water treated by the accident are different.
