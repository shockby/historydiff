Goguryeo (37 BC - 668 BC) was an ancient state that existed for approximately 700 years from present-day Northeastern China to the northern part of the Korean Peninsula.

In Japanese academic circles, there is a growing tendency to view Goguryeo as a unique wide-area state in Northeast Asia that cannot simply be attributed to either Chinese or Korean history. In Japanese high school world history textbooks, Goguryeo is generally introduced in the context of the ``Three Kingdoms Period on the Korean Peninsula,'' but it is also mentioned that its dominion area expanded widely into what is now the northeastern region of China.

The following points have been pointed out by Japanese scholars of Oriental history.

The first is the limitations of discussing the attribution of ancient nations within modern borders. Goguryeo was a nation at a time when the current national borders did not exist, and there is a view that the binary debate of ``Chinese history or Korean history'' itself is an issue based on modern nationalism. The cultural and political system of Goguryeo included a complex of influences from the Chungwon Dynasty, unique nomadic elements, and cultural elements from the Korean Peninsula.

Second, there is the **relationship with ancient Japanese history**. Goguryeo is not unrelated to Japan either. The Nihon Shoki records records of diplomatic relations with Koguryo, and it is known that immigrants from Koguryo influenced Japan's culture and technology. Koma Shrine in Hidaka City, Saitama Prefecture is said to be dedicated to Jakko, a member of the royal family of Goguryeo, and is a reminder of the existence of Goguryeo survivors who immigrated to Japan after the fall of Goguryeo.

Thirdly, there is **Japan's academic interest in the Tohoku Process**. Although the Japanese government has not expressed an official position regarding China's Northeast Project, Japanese historians and East Asian researchers are closely monitoring this controversy. Since China's theory of a unified multi-ethnic state applies not only to Goguryeo but also to the history of Balhai, Tibet, and Uyghur, it is being academically analyzed as an issue that could affect the entire historical understanding of East Asia.

Japan's position, from a third-party perspective that does not subscribe to the claims of either China or South Korea, points out the problems with understanding ancient nations within the framework of modern nation-states, and tends to place Goguryeo within the pluralistic history of Northeast Asia.
