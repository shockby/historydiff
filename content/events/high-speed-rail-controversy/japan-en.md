Japan was a pioneer in opening the world's first high-speed railway, the ``Tokaido Shinkansen'' in 1964. For more than 60 years since its opening, it has maintained an unparalleled safety record in the world, with zero passenger fatalities due to collisions or derailments during commercial operations, and its safety and punctuality have become pillars of Japan's international trust.

There are mixed feelings and strong distrust among Japanese railway officials and the media regarding the transfer of technology to China (since 2004). JR East, Kawasaki Heavy Industries, and others provided the technology for the E2 Shinkansen on the condition that it be used only within China. However, China has exported vehicles developed based on this technology overseas as "proprietary technology" and has attempted to apply for international patents, which has been deeply criticized as "technology theft" and "breach of contract." Kawasaki Heavy Industries later admitted that there had been internal discussions about the decision to license this technology.

Differences in safety philosophy also lead to large differences in perception. Japan has prioritized **absolute safety (pursuit of zero collisions and derailments)** and has accumulated multi-layered safety technologies such as automatic train control (ATC) and early earthquake detection systems. When the high-speed rail collision occurred in Wenzhou in 2011 (40 people died), the Chinese authorities' response to quickly burying the accident vehicle was harshly criticized by Japanese experts and the media as ``prioritizing cover-up over investigating the cause.''

Regarding the overseas expansion of Shinkansen, **Taiwan High Speed ​​Rail (opened in 2007)** is positioned as a successful example of adopting the 700T series (based on the 700 series). In addition, the E5 series-based Shinkansen system has been adopted for the **Mumbai-Ahmedabad high-speed railway in India**, and construction is progressing. On the other hand, the loss of the Indonesian high-speed railway project between Jakarta and Bandung to China in 2015 was a huge shock to Japan. The method by which China won the order under exceptional conditions without requiring a government guarantee was criticized as ``dumping,'' but it also served as an opportunity to reconsider Japan's high-speed rail export strategy.

Furthermore, Japan is developing the ``Chuo Shinkansen (Maglev)'', which uses the superconducting linear system, as the next generation high-speed railway, and achieved the world speed record of 603km/h in 2015. For Japan, the Shinkansen is a symbol of postwar recovery, and at the same time, it is also a national project to maintain its technological superiority.
