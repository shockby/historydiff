Since its opening in 2004, the Korean High Speed ​​Rail (KTX) has dramatically changed the country's transportation network and steadily advanced the development of unique technology.

During the introduction stage (1990s), South Korea compared Japan's Shinkansen, France's TGV, and Germany's ICE, but ultimately chose the French TGV. The biggest reason for this is that France has promised more aggressive "technology transfer" than Japan. The understanding at the time was that Japan was reluctant to disclose its core technology, while France was flexible in producing domestically in Korea and transferring technical know-how. It is also said that historical sentiments surrounding Japan's colonial rule, as well as diplomatic relations at the time and favorable financial conditions on the French side, influenced the selection.

After the opening of KTX, South Korea promoted **domestic production** based on the introduced technology. In 2010, we put the independently developed KTX-Sancheon into commercial operation, and in 2013 we successfully completed a driving test with the test vehicle **HEMU-430X**, which recorded a speed of 430km/h. In 2016, a privately operated high-speed railway **SRT (Super Rapid Train)**, which is separate from KTX, opened, realizing the diversification of high-speed rail services.

South Korea is actively seeking to expand into overseas markets based on its own technological capabilities. However, looking at Japan's experience in losing Indonesia's Jakarta-Bandung high-speed railway to China, Korean media have begun to discuss ``what will be lost in return for technology transfer.''

For South Korea, the high-speed railway is a symbol of its joining the ranks of developed countries, and it is a symbol of the country's industrial strength, which has grown from a former ``technology-adopting country'' to a ``technology-developing and exporting country.''
