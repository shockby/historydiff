In 1956, against the backdrop of the international situation following the criticism of Stalin, Mao Zedong launched the policy of ``Hundred Flowers Free, Hundred Houses Fight'' and encouraged intellectuals to speak freely. Initially, it was meant to encourage constructive criticism of the Chinese Communist Party, but when the actual criticism leveled against the party's one-party dictatorship itself, Mao Zedong completely changed his stance.

In June 1957, the Communist Party launched an ``anti-right-wing struggle'' and denounced intellectuals, cultural figures, and Democratic Party figures who criticized the party as ``right-wing elements'' one after another. Officially, approximately 550,000 people were identified as right-wing, but the actual number of victims is believed to be much higher. Those deemed right-wing were forced to quit their jobs, be sent to rural areas or undergo labor reorganization, and some were even driven to suicide. Famous writer Ding Ling and sociologist Fei Hyo-tong were also considered right-wing.

The prevailing view is that the sudden change from the Hundred Flowers Movement to an anti-right-wing struggle was a ``ploy'' (trap) by Mao Zedong to deliberately lure intellectuals and suppress them. As a result of this series of movements, China's intellectual class was forced to severely atrophy, and from then on it became extremely difficult to voice criticism of the authorities. The anti-rightist struggle set a precedent for the subsequent control of speech during the Great Leap Forward and the Cultural Revolution, and is said to have formed the prototype of ideological oppression in modern Chinese history.
