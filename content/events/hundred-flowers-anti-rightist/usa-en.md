In 1956, Communist Party dictator Mao Zedong encouraged intellectuals to openly criticize the Communist Party under the slogan "One Hundred Flowers, One Hundred Families, One Hundred Families." Although it ostensibly promised free speech, its true purpose is still debated. One popular theory is that Mao Zedong set up a clever trap (a ``ploy'') to expose dissident intellectuals from the beginning.

In 1957, Mao launched an "anti-rightist struggle" as criticism turned unexpectedly on the Communist Party's one-party rule itself. An estimated 500,000 or more citizens were labeled "right-wing", stripped of their jobs, sent to forced labor camps, or exiled to remote rural villages. Many intellectuals, writers, scientists, and students were targeted, and their families were socially wiped out. Their "crime" was simply expressing an honest opinion to the government.

The anti-right-wing struggle is a classic example of how communist totalitarian regimes are incompatible with freedom of speech. This movement instilled deep fear and self-censorship in Chinese society, creating a situation where no one could speak out against the catastrophic failure of the later Great Leap Forward (an estimated 15 million to 55 million people died of starvation). The anti-right-wing struggle is important as a historical lesson in how the suppression of political dissent can have devastating consequences for an entire country.
