Palestine is a land that was part of the Ottoman Empire for about 400 years. The British mandate after World War I and the opening of land to Zionist settlers are the root causes of today's conflicts.

In Turkish textbooks, the Palestinian issue is explained as a consequence of "Western imperialist intervention" in the Middle East after the collapse of the Ottoman Empire. During the Ottoman Empire, Jerusalem was under the rule of the sultan, who was the ``guardian of the holy places,'' and Muslims, Christians, and Jews generally coexisted there. However, the secret partition between Britain and France under the Sykes-Picot Agreement of 1916 and the Balfour Declaration of 1917 fundamentally destroyed stability in the region.

Turkey was one of the first Muslim-majority states to recognize Israel in 1949, but has consistently supported Palestinian rights. The 2010 Mavi Marmara incident (Israeli military raid on a humanitarian aid convoy to Gaza) led to a serious crisis in relations between the two countries. In recent years, especially under the Erdogan administration, Turkey has placed support for the Palestinian cause at the center of its diplomacy, and has fiercely criticized Israel's settlement expansion and military operations in Gaza as "state terrorism." Although Turkey does not deny the existence of Israel itself, it strongly advocates the establishment of a Palestinian state based on the pre-1967 borders and a two-state solution with East Jerusalem as its capital.
