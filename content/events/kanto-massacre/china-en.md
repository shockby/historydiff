On September 1, 1923, a major earthquake struck the Kanto region of Japan. In the ensuing chaos, various rumors about Koreans spread rapidly, and Japanese vigilante groups, the military, and the police carried out mass killings of Koreans. The massacre is believed to have killed **more than 6,000 Koreans**, while Chinese residents in Japan were also subjected to the same persecution, with several hundred fatalities among them.

Chinese textbooks and media place this incident in the historical context of Japanese imperialist racial discrimination and colonial violence, treating it as a historical event that reveals the essence of modern Japanese nativism. The fact that victims included not only Koreans but also Chinese residents in Japan is a point of particular significance to China. Chinese media (including People's Daily and Xinhua) reported critically on the Japanese government's refusal to officially acknowledge the massacre at the 100th anniversary in 2023, characterizing it as an example of Japanese historical revisionism.

While the Chinese government has issued no specific official statement on the matter, the incident is invoked in the broader context of criticizing Japan's attitude toward its history. Chinese historians also address this incident in research on modern Japanese history and imperialism, analyzing it as an important case for understanding nativist ideology and state violence in modern Japan.
