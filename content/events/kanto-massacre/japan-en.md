In the chaos following the Great Kanto Earthquake (magnitude 7.9) on September 1, 1923, rumors spread that "Koreans had poisoned wells" and "Koreans were rioting," leading to the killing of large numbers of Korean residents by vigilante groups, the military, and police. Chinese residents and socialists were also targeted.

In Japanese textbooks, this incident is often mentioned only briefly. While some texts note that "many Koreans were killed due to rumors," details regarding the scale, organized nature, and state responsibility are frequently omitted. Even at the 100th anniversary of the earthquake on September 1, 2023, the Japanese government (Chief Cabinet Secretary Hirokazu Matsuno) avoided the term "massacre" and declined to officially recognize the number of victims or the state's organized involvement, stating that "no records confirming the facts can be found by the government."

In Tokyo, Governor Yuriko Koike has refrained from sending a condolence message to the Korean victims' memorial ceremony since 2017, citing that "there are various theories about the historical facts." However, a memorial monument stands in Yokoamicho Park (Sumida Ward, Tokyo), and annual memorial services continue to be held. In Japanese academic circles, research by figures such as Yoshino Sakuzo at the time and postwar scholars Akitsugi Yamada and Masao Nishizaki is well-established, with the widely supported view that more than 6,000 people were killed.
