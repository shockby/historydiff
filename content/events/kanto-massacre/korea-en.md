In the chaos following the Great Kanto Earthquake of September 1, 1923, baseless rumors rapidly spread that "Koreans had poisoned wells" and "Koreans were setting fires and rioting." Using these as a pretext, Japanese vigilante groups, military forces, and police systematically massacred **more than 6,000 Koreans**.

South Korean textbooks clearly characterize this incident as a tragic consequence of Japanese imperialist colonial rule and racial discrimination. At the time, Koreans living under colonial rule had migrated to Japan and were living in harsh conditions; in the crisis of the earthquake, they became scapegoats. The massacre was not merely the spontaneous action of vigilante groups — the military and police directly participated or tacitly approved, giving it the character of state violence.

The South Korean government has consistently demanded that the Japanese government officially acknowledge the facts of the "massacre" and issue an apology. At the 100th anniversary in 2023, the South Korean government intensified its own investigation into the truth of the events and requested the disclosure of related official documents from Japan. Within South Korea, Korean-Japanese organizations, academic institutions, and civil society have continued commemorative and historical documentation activities. This incident is taught as a historical fact that reveals the violent nature of Japanese imperial colonial rule and the essence of racial discrimination.
