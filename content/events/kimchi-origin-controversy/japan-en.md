Kimchi (Korean: 김치) is a fermented food made by pickling vegetables such as Chinese cabbage with condiments such as chili peppers, garlic, and fish sauce, and is one of the most popular foods in Japan.

In Japan, kimchi is generally recognized as a **Korean traditional food**. Kimchi is always on display in the pickle section of supermarkets, and there are also many uniquely Japanese dishes such as ``kimchi hotpot'' and ``pork kimchi.'' It is said that the amount of kimchi consumed in Japan is second only to that of South Korea, and it is deeply ingrained in Japanese food culture.

The Japanese media mainly reports and analyzes the controversy over the origin of kimchi between China and South Korea from the following perspectives.

Firstly, the historical background of the introduction of chili peppers. In research on the history of Japanese food culture, it is widely known that chili peppers were introduced to the Korean Peninsula at the end of the 16th century to the beginning of the 17th century, around the time of Toyotomi Hideyoshi's invasion of Korea (Bunroku and Keicho War). Chili peppers are originally from South America, and are said to have come to Japan via Portugal, and then from Japan to the Korean Peninsula. In other words, kimchi as we know it today, which uses red chili peppers, is a relatively new food that was created after the 17th century, and before that, pickles (sincho) on the Korean Peninsula did not use chili peppers. This historical fact has been pointed out by Japanese researchers as important background information in the debate over the origins of kimchi.

Second, there is the perspective of **the diversity of pickle culture in East Asia**. Japan also has a long tradition of pickles, and a variety of fermented pickles such as rice bran pickles, miso pickles, and lees pickles have been developed. Among Japanese food culture researchers, there is a view that China's foamed greens, Korean kimchi, and Japanese pickles developed uniquely according to their respective climates, ingredients, and eating habits, and that rather than arguing over a single "origin," they should be understood as the richness of the fermented food culture of East Asia as a whole.

Thirdly, **the history surrounding the Japanese kimchi market**. In the 1990s and 2000s, there was some controversy between Japan and South Korea over the difference between the lightly pickled "kimchi-style pickles" produced in Japan and traditional Korean kimchi. The establishment of the Codex standard (CODEX STAN 223-2001) in 2001 was partly due to this debate between Japan and South Korea over "what is authentic kimchi," and Japan was also involved in the process of formulating this international standard.

Japan's position is not to be a party to it, but to objectively observe the dispute between China and South Korea, but as a historical intermediary in the introduction of chili peppers and a member of the East Asian pickle culture community, Japan offers its own unique perspective on this controversy.
