Kimchi (김치) is a traditional fermented food that represents Korean food culture and is an indispensable part of Koreans' daily diet.

According to Korean historical understanding, kimchi is a food culture unique to Korea, and is clearly distinguished from China's pao tsai in terms of manufacturing method, ingredients, and cultural meaning. The main arguments of the Korean side are as follows.

Firstly, **the uniqueness of the manufacturing method and fermentation process**. Chinese foam cabbage is a relatively simple pickle that is mainly pickled in salt water and fermented with lactic acid, but Korean kimchi is made by pickling Chinese cabbage in salt and then fermenting it with complex condiments such as chili powder, garlic, ginger, salted fish sauce (젓갈), and fish sauce. This unique recipe and complex flavor are unique to Korea, having developed over hundreds of years.

Second is the uniqueness of the Kimjang culture. ``Kimjang'' (김장), in which families and local communities make large amounts of kimchi every late fall, is an important traditional event that symbolizes the communal culture of Korean society. In 2013, UNESCO registered this ``Kimjang, the culture of soaking and sharing kimchi'' as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. This means that not the food itself, but the Korean community culture centered around kimchi, has been internationally recognized.

Third, **Rebuttal to misunderstandings surrounding ISO standards**. The South Korean government has issued a statement that ISO 24220, which was established in 2020, is a standard for "Pao cai", or Sichuan-style pickles, and **Korean kimchi is clearly excluded**. Kimchi already has its own international standard ``CODEX STAN 223-2001'' established by the International Food Standards Committee (Codex Alimentarius) in 2001, and kimchi and foamed cabbage are internationally recognized as different foods.

The Korean government has positioned protecting the origin and uniqueness of kimchi as an important cultural policy issue, and through the Ministry of Culture, Sports, and Tourism and the Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry, Livestock, and Food is actively working to promote and protect kimchi culture internationally.
