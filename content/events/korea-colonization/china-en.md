In Chinese history textbooks, Japan's colonial rule of Korea is taught as part of the Japanese imperialist invasion and control system in East Asia. The Treaty of Shimonoseki during the **Sino-Japanese War** (1894-95), in which Japan eliminated the suzerainty relationship with Korea from Qing Dynasty, and Japan's victory in the **Russo-Japanese War** (1904-05) eliminated Russia's interests in Korea, can be described as imperialist expansion in which Japan gradually deprived Korea of ​​its sovereignty. The acquiescence of the United States in the **Katsura-Taft Memorandum** (1905) and the retreat of Russia in the **Treaty of Portsmouth** are positioned as international contexts in which Korea (Empire of Korea), now isolated and helpless, loses its diplomatic means.

**As for the implications of Japanese rule for China**, Chinese textbooks emphasize that the railroads, ports, and military infrastructure built in Korea served as the logistics base for the subsequent invasion of mainland China (**1931 Manchurian Incident** and **1937 Sino-Japanese War**). The Japanese military's stationing, resource extraction, and labor exploitation in Korea was part of an imperialist system that continued with similar policies of aggression against China, and Koreans and Chinese were seen as common victims. The **March First Movement** of 1919** is sometimes referred to as a pioneer of anti-imperialist and national liberation struggles in Asia, along with China's **May Fourth Movement** of the same year.

The Chinese government takes the position that a prerequisite for regional reconciliation and stability is for Japan to sincerely acknowledge its history of colonial rule and apologize. Visits to Yasukuni Shrine, revisionist tendencies in history textbooks, and arguments for revising the Kono Statement have been consistently criticized by the Chinese government, and the issue of historical recognition continues to be a diplomatic issue in China-Japan relations.
