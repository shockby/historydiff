**The background to the annexation of Japan and Korea (1910)** is that after the Sino-Japanese War (1894-95) eliminated Qing suzerainty over the Korean Peninsula, the independence and modernization of Korea became an urgent need. The Korean Empire (founded in 1897) under Gojong (Emperor Gwangmu) carried out the Gwangmu Reform and attempted independent modernization, but the political situation was unstable and the country continued to be interfered with by the great powers. To counter Russia's move southward, Japan fought the Russo-Japanese War (1904-05), and the Treaty of Portsmouth (1905) eliminated Russia's right to interfere in its superior position in Korea. In the same year, Japan seized Korea's diplomatic rights through the Katsura-Taft Memorandum (mutual understanding with the United States) and the Second Japan-Korea Treaty (Eulsa Treaty), abdicated Gojong in **1907**, and officially incorporated the Korean Peninsula into its territory through the Japan-Korea Annexation Treaty of **August 22, 1910**.

Regarding **aspects of Japan's governance and modernization**, Japanese history textbooks sometimes mention things like the development of railways, roads, ports, and communication networks (the total length of railroads in Korea expanded significantly during the rule period), the introduction of a modern school system, the improvement of average life expectancy through the development of medical and sanitary administration, and the expansion of agricultural productivity. On the other hand, in recent years there has been an increasing number of descriptions of the loss of land by farmers due to the land survey project (1910-1918), the extortion of food due to the rice production plan, the change of Korean names to Japanese names, the prohibition of Korean language education, and wartime labor mobilization and military conscription. In academia, the majority of research has found that the benefits of modernization have been biased primarily toward Japanese immigrants and the ruling class, while Korean farmers and workers have been treated differently.

Regarding the **government opinion**, in the **Murayama Statement** in 1995, Japan expressed ``deep remorse'' and ``apology'' for ``colonial rule and invasion.'' In 2010, Prime Minister Naoto Kan's statement clearly stated that colonial rule was carried out "against the will of the people at the time." The Japanese government takes the position that the issue of property and claims has been ``completely and finally resolved'' by the 1965 **Japan-Korea Basic Treaty** and the Claims Agreement, and there is an ongoing gap between the government's view and some conservative and revisionist commentators regarding historical understanding.
