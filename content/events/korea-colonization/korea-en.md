In Korean history education, Japan's colonial rule is positioned as the "**Japanese Occupation Period** (일제강점기)." The Korean Empire, which was established in **1897**, aimed for autonomous modernization through the ``Gwangmu Reform'' under **Gojong** (Emperor Gwangmu), but Japan's victory in the Russo-Japanese War (1904-05) drastically changed the fate of Korea. Diplomatic rights were revoked by the Eulsa Treaty (Second Japan-Korea Treaty) of **1905**, and Gojong dispatched an emissary to the Universal Peace Conference in The Hague (1907) to complain of unfairness, but it was ignored and he was forced to abdicate. After that, **Sunjong** ascended the throne, but had no real power, and Korea's sovereignty was completely abolished by the forced annexation in **1910**. Japan's colonial rule was not what the Korean people desired, but was imposed through military and diplomatic coercion.

**As a critical evaluation of the ``modernization'' advocated by the Japanese side,** South Korean history textbooks and academic circles evaluate that the introduction of infrastructure development and educational systems was not ``modernization for the benefit of the Korean people,'' but was aimed at streamlining Japan's expropriation system and making Korea a logistics base for its invasion of the continent. Many farmers lost their land during the **Land Survey Project** (1910-1918), and most of the rice produced during the **Rice Production Project** (1920-1934) was shipped to Japan, worsening the food situation for Koreans. Koreans were subjected to discriminatory treatment in all aspects of education, administration, and economics, and the benefits of modernization mainly belonged to Japanese immigrants.

Approximately **2 million people** participated in the **March First Independence Movement** (1919) across the country, and many were killed and injured due to oppression by the Japanese military police and military. The imperialization policy from the 1930s onward prohibited Korean language education, forced people to change their names, and forced them to worship at shrines. During the Pacific War, large-scale **recruitment (forced labor mobilization)** and conscription took place, and women were mobilized to serve as ``comfort women'' in the Japanese military. The South Korean government maintains its position that the 1965 Claims Agreement does not include compensation for individual human rights violations, and continues to demand a sincere apology from Japan and compensation to victims.
