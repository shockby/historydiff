As for the history of U.S. diplomacy and the loss of Korea, the 1905 Katsura–Taft Agreement is a particularly important diplomatic context. This informal understanding between William Taft (then Secretary of War) and Prime Minister Katsura Taro recognized Japan's "suzerainty" over Korea in exchange for the United States' control over the Philippines. The Treaty of Portsmouth (1905), mediated by President Theodore Roosevelt, indirectly facilitated recognition of Japan's superior status in Korea. Even when Gojong appealed to the international community through the Hague Emissary (1907), the United States did not provide effective support. American academic circles have critically evaluated this as ``diplomatic acquiescence'' to the loss of Korean independence.

**As an academic argument for "colonial modernization theory," research by Bruce Cummings (University of Chicago) and others analyzes the fact that Japan's colonial rule brought a certain level of infrastructure development, industrialization, and education to Korea, but also clearly shows that the benefits were more unevenly distributed to Japanese immigrants and capitalists than to Koreans, that extractive land and food policies impoverished the peasant class, and that forced cultural assimilation suppressed ethnic identity. In mainstream American academic circles, the view that ``modernization'' during the Japanese colonial period is the basis for justifying Japanese rule is not mainstream.

**As for the US position** in modern times, the US Congress adopted **House Resolution 121** (submitted by Rep. Honda) in 2007, calling on the Japanese government to issue an official apology to the Japanese military's "comfort women." Although the U.S. government regards the historical issue between Japan and South Korea as a bilateral issue, it has consistently promoted historical reconciliation between Japan and South Korea from the perspective of stabilizing the Korean Peninsula and the triangular alliance between Japan, the United States, and South Korea.
