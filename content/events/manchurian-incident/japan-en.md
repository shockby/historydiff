On September 18, 1931, the South Manchurian Railway's tracks were blown up near Liutiao Lake on the outskirts of Mukden (now Shenyang) (the Liutiao Lake Incident). The Kwantung Army assumed this was the work of China and began military operations, occupying all of Manchuria.

In Japanese textbooks, the Manchuria Incident is described as an arbitrary action by the Kwantung Army. At the time, Japan was facing a severe economic recession due to the Great Depression, and the preservation of Japan's interests in Manchuria (South Manchuria Railway, Kanto Province leased land, etc.) became an important issue. Current textbooks also mention that the Liujo Lake Incident was a self-made act by some officers of the Kwantung Army.

Manchukuo was founded in 1932, and although it advocated the ideals of "harmonious harmony among the five tribes" and "royal paradise," in reality it was a nation under strong Japanese influence. The League of Nations did not recognize Manchukuo based on the Lytton Survey's report, and Japan withdrew from the League of Nations in 1933. This series of events is considered to be the turning point where Japan deviated from the path of international cooperation and began a path toward isolation.
