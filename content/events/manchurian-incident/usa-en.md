In 1931, the Japanese army began military operations in Manchuria (northeast China) and occupied this region. This was the first major case of aggression in Asia leading to World War II.

In American textbooks, the Manchuria Incident is positioned as an important event that marked the collapse of the international order in the 1930s. Japan's Kwantung Army used the railway bombing as a pretext to carry out a full-scale military occupation, an act that clearly violated the Washington regime and the Treaty of No War (Kellogg-Briand Treaty).

U.S. Secretary of State Henry Stimson declared the ``Stimson Doctrine'' and took the position that he would not recognize territorial changes by force. However, this statement was not accompanied by military or economic coercion, and could not effectively prevent Japan's actions. The League of Nations only sent a Lytton investigation team and made recommendations, and Japan responded by withdrawing. This series of events serves as a historical lesson in the limits of collective security arrangements and the dangers of appeasement leading to larger-scale invasions.
