On July 7, 1937, the Japanese invasion force launched a provocative attack on the Chinese garrison near the Marco Polo Bridge in the southwest of Beiping under the pretext of military exercises. This was the ``77 Incident (Lugou Bridge Incident),'' and Japan officially launched a full-scale war of aggression against China.

In Chinese textbooks, the Marco Polo Bridge Incident is clearly positioned as a military provocation intentionally provoked by Japan. In the first place, the fact that the Japanese military is conducting military exercises near the Chinese capital, Beiping, is in itself a gross violation of China's sovereignty and an indication of its intent to invade. They demanded access to Chinese military positions under the pretext of ``searching for missing soldiers,'' and when their request was refused, they launched an armed attack.

The Chinese garrison (29th Army) bravely resisted, and the vice commander, Li Linge, and the commander, Zhao Dengyu, were killed in action, but their sacrifices are praised as a symbol of the indomitable spirit of the Chinese nation. In the wake of the 1977 Incident, China established an all-ethnic resistance system, and the second joint effort was established. This day marks the beginning of the eight-year all-out war of resistance against Japan by the Chinese people.
