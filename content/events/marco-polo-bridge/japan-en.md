On the night of July 7, 1937, several shots were fired at Japanese troops during an exercise near the Marco Polo Bridge on the outskirts of Beiping (present-day Beijing). This conflict escalated into a full-scale armed conflict between Japan and China.

In Japanese textbooks, the Marco Polo Bridge Incident is described relatively objectively as a ``conflict between the Japanese and Chinese forces.'' To this day, it has not been determined who fired the first shot. After the incident, the Japanese government initially announced a "non-expansion policy," but the situation became uncontrollable as there were repeated clashes on the ground and incidents such as the Tongzhou Incident (the murder of a Japanese resident) occurred.

The full-scale armed conflict that followed was called the ``China Incident'' in Japan at the time. The textbook explains that one of the reasons why a declaration of war was not made was that in the event of a formal war, America's neutrality laws would be applied, and there was a risk that imports of goods would be hindered. The prolongation of this incident exhausted Japan's national power and became a major factor leading to the Pacific War.
