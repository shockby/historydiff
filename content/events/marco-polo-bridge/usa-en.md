In July 1937, an armed conflict broke out between the Chinese and Chinese armies near the Marco Polo Bridge near Beijing. As a result of this incident, the local conflict between Japan and China escalated into a full-scale war.

In American textbooks, the Marco Polo Bridge Incident is positioned as an extension of Japan's continental expansion policy, which began with the Manchurian Incident in 1931. There are also accounts that see the legacy of unequal treaties and an imperialist structure in the very fact that the Japanese military claimed the right to conduct military exercises near the Chinese capital.

The all-out war between Japan and China that followed the incident was officially known as the "undeclared war." American textbooks depict the process in which the United States initially took a neutral position, avoiding direct intervention, but domestic public opinion turned anti-Japanese in the wake of Japan's invasion of China (particularly the Nanjing Massacre and the Panay Incident). President Roosevelt's Quarantine Speech (October 1937) is noted as an important turning point in which he advocated the isolation of aggressor countries such as Japan.
