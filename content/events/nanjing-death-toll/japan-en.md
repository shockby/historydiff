Regarding the number of victims of the Nanjing Incident, Japanese textbooks generally do not specify specific numbers, and tend to limit themselves to expressions such as ``many'' and ``a large amount.''

Some textbooks include an annotation that says, ``There are many different views on the number of victims, and debate continues even today.'' The Japanese government's official position (Ministry of Foreign Affairs) states that ``it cannot be denied that non-combatants were killed and looted,'' but it also takes the position that ``there are various theories as to the exact number of victims, and it is difficult for the government to certify an official number.''

In Japanese historical academia, there are three main views regarding the number of victims. The **genocide faction** (Jukushi Kasahara, Tomio Hora, etc.) estimates the number of people to be 100,000 to 200,000, the **intermediate faction** (Ikuhiko Hata, etc.) estimates the number to be about 40,000, and the **fiction faction** (Shu Higashinakano, Masaaki Tanaka, etc.) questions the systematic genocide itself.

Japanese researchers cite the following points as reasons why the number of victims cannot be determined. First, there is a difference in the **geographical range** (limited to Nanjing City or including six surrounding prefectures) that are counted as victims. Second, there is a difference in the **temporal range** (a few weeks immediately after the occupation or until around March 1938). Thirdly, there is the issue of ``definition of target'', whether or not the execution of disarmed and defeated soldiers is included in ``genocide''. It is said that differences in these assumptions can lead to different conclusions being drawn from the same material.
