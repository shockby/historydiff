Regarding the number of victims of the Nanjing Massacre, Taiwan (Republic of China) has historically taken the same position as mainland China, **more than 300,000**, but the focus of the description has changed due to recent educational reforms.

During the period of single-party rule by the Kuomintang (until the 1990s), Taiwanese textbooks contained descriptions similar to mainland Chinese textbooks, specifying the number of victims of over 300,000 people based on the findings of the Nanjing Military Tribunal. This is because the government of the Republic of China itself sponsored the Nanking Military Tribunal, and the Nanjing Massacre was deeply tied to the identity of the Republic of China as an event that symbolized the suffering of the Chinese nation during the War of Resistance.

However, with the progress of democratization and Taiwanese independence (mainlandization), textbook revisions since the 2000s have shifted the focus of history education from "Chinese history" to "Taiwanese history." Current textbooks treat the Nanjing Incident in the context of modern East Asian history, but they tend not to go into as much detail about the number of victims as before.

In Taiwanese academia, scholarly examination of the number of casualties is carried out more freely than in mainland China. The Institute of Modern History, Academia Sinica, and other institutions are organizing and releasing the primary documents (drafts) of the Nanjing Military Tribunal, and academic evaluation of the evidence submitted to the court is also a subject of open discussion. However, the mainstream of Taiwanese historical scholarship has shifted to research into Taiwan's own modern and contemporary history, and the debate over the number of victims of the Nanjing Incident is no longer a central theme in Taiwanese academic circles.
