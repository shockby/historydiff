In December 1937, the Japanese army occupied Nanjing, the capital of the Republic of China, during the expansion of the Sino-Japanese War.

In Japanese textbooks, this incident is commonly referred to as the ``Nanjing Incident.'' The descriptions are generally concise, and are often mentioned in the form of ``When the Japanese army occupied Nanjing, they committed numerous acts of murder and looting against prisoners of war and civilians (Nanjing Incident)''. Most textbooks do not specify specific numbers regarding the number of victims, and tend to limit themselves to the expression "many."

Some textbooks include an annotation stating, ``There are various opinions regarding the number of victims in this incident, and debate continues even today.'' It may also be mentioned that at the Tokyo Trial (International Military Tribunal for the Far East), General Matsui Iwane, the commander at the time of the capture of Nanking, was held responsible and executed. Overall, while acknowledging the existence of the incident, details about its scale and nature have been refrained.
