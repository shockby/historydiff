After World War II, as part of the Allied war crimes trial, the government of the Republic of China set up military courts in various parts of China, including Nanjing, to try BC class war criminals of the Japanese military.

In Japanese textbooks, there is almost no mention of the Nanjing Military Tribunal itself, and they tend to focus on the **Tokyo Trial (International Military Tribunal for the Far East)** as a postwar war crimes trial. Although it is sometimes mentioned that General Matsui Iwane was convicted and executed for neglecting his duty to prevent atrocities in Nanjing at the Tokyo Trials, many textbooks do not mention the trial of Lieutenant General Hisao Tani at the Nanjing Military Tribunal.

In Japanese academic circles, there are various opinions regarding the Nanjing Military Tribunal's procedures and handling of evidence. Regarding the number of victims determined by the court to be ``over 300,000,'' it has been pointed out that there was a problem with the evidence verification process. In particular, points of contention include the possibility of double counting of burial records, insufficient verification of the reliability of witness testimony, and limited opportunities for the defense to disprove evidence. Furthermore, with regard to the ``Hyakunin Slash Competition'' that was judged in court, it is being debated that the military service article in the Tokyo Nichiichi Shimbun, which served as the basis for the case, may have been an exaggeration or a fabrication to boost fighting spirit, and the bereaved families have filed a lawsuit to restore honor after the war.

On the other hand, opinions are divided, with some criticizing the entire postwar war crimes trials, including the Nanjing Military Tribunal, as ``victors' judgments,'' while others recognize the historical significance of the trial as a forum for holding people accountable for the atrocities committed by Japan.
