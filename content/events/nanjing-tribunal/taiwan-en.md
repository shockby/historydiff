From 1946 to 1947, the government of the Republic of China (Kuomintang government) established the Ministry of National Defense Tribunal War Crimes Military Tribunal in the capital, Nanjing, and conducted war crimes trials for war crimes committed by the Japanese military, including the Nanjing Massacre.

For Taiwan, this tribunal has special historical significance as a war crimes trial sponsored by the Republic of China government. During the Kuomintang one-party period (~1990s), the Nanjing Massacre and the Nanjing Military Tribunal were described in detail in textbooks. The court's death sentence against Lieutenant General Hisao Tani was seen as proof that the Republic of China took a firm stance against Japanese aggression, and textbooks included recognition of 300,000 victims. History education during this period emphasized the leading role and sacrifices of the Kuomintang (government of the Republic of China) in the War of Resistance against Japan.

However, since the 2000s, when Taiwan's democratization and mainlandization (emphasis on Taiwanese independence) progressed, the composition of history textbooks has changed significantly. The center of gravity has shifted from the traditional "history of mainland China" to "history of Taiwan," and descriptions of the Nanjing Incident and the Nanjing Military Tribunal tend to be simpler than before. In current textbooks, these events are treated as part of modern East Asian history, but the tone that once emphasized the realization of justice by the Republic of China has faded.

In Taiwan's academic community, the Institute of Modern History of the Academia Sinica and other institutions are organizing and digitizing archives (official documents) related to war crimes trials. Academic verification is carried out more freely than in mainland China, and there is an environment where open discussion can take place regarding court procedures and the handling of evidence. However, the study of the Nanjing Incident itself is moving away from the mainstream theme of Taiwan's historical academic community, and the focus of research has shifted to Taiwan's own modern and contemporary history.
