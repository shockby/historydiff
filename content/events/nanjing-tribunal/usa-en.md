After the end of World War II, the Allies set up military tribunals to try Japanese war crimes. The most widely known case is the Tokyo Trials (IMTFE), but the Republic of China government also independently conducted military trials of BC-class war criminals in various parts of China, including Nanjing.

In American textbooks, the postwar war crimes trials against Japan are mainly described in the context of the **Tokyo Trials (International Military Tribunal for the Far East)**. There are few textbooks that detail the Nanking Military Tribunal (Military Tribunal for Military Crimes Tribunal by the Ministry of National Defense) as an independent topic, and they mainly focus on the fact that the Rape of Nanking was one of the main charges in the Tokyo Tribunal.

In American academic research, the Nanjing Military Tribunal is positioned as part of the postwar war crimes trial system. The number of victims certified by the court as ``more than 300,000'' is referred to as one of several estimates, but it needs to be understood in light of the limitations of the estimation method, along with the finding of ``more than 200,000'' in the Tokyo trial. The fact that Americans who were staying in Nanjing at the time (Professor Minor Searle Bates of Jinling University, Reverend John McGee, etc.) testified as witnesses in this court also indicates an indirect connection between the United States and the court.

As the Cold War intensified, America's policy toward Japan shifted from "punishment" to "reconstruction and alliance." This policy change also affected the unfinished cases of the Tokyo Trials, contributing to the decline in interest in the war crimes trials as a whole. The reason why the Nanjing Military Tribunal has less international recognition than the Tokyo Trials is also related to these Cold War political dynamics.
