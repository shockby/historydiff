In August 1945, Japan faced an unprecedented situation: the world's first atomic bombs were dropped on Hiroshima (6th) and Nagasaki (9th), and the Soviet Union entered the war.

Japanese textbooks take seriously the fact that the dropping of the atomic bomb instantly claimed the lives of tens of thousands of civilians, and that survivors continue to suffer from radiation sickness long after the war, and emphasize the aspect of the bomb as an "inhumane weapon." It will also be discussed in detail how the Soviet Union's entry into the war was a decisive factor that led the military to make the final decision that ``continuation of the war was impossible.''

On August 15th, the Japanese people were informed of the acceptance of the Potsdam Declaration and the defeat of the war through a radio broadcast (Gyokuon Broadcasting) by Emperor Showa. Along with the phrase, ``endure the unbearable, endure the unbearable,'' this is depicted as a painful decision to avoid the extinction of the nation. The memory of this disaster is the origin of postwar Japan's pacifism and the Three Non-Nuclear Principles, which call for a ``world without nuclear weapons.''
