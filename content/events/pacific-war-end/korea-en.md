On August 15, 1945, the day of independence for the Republic of Korea arrived. This is the moment when Japan's colonial rule, which lasted for 35 years since 1910, came to an end, and it is a memorable day when the unyielding anti-Japanese independence movement finally came to fruition.

In Korean textbooks, the Allied powers' dropping of atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki and the entry of the Soviet Union into the war are portrayed as the direct cause of Japan's unconditional surrender, which led to the immediate "liberation" of Korea. However, it is emphasized that this was not just a matter of good fortune due to external factors, but a ``victory of the nation'' that was achieved because of the continuous military and political resistance activities of the provisional government in Shanghai, the Korean Liberation Army, and others.

On the other hand, the division of the Korean peninsula by the United States and the Soviet Union at the 38th parallel immediately after Japan's surrender is also described as the tragic cost of ``heteronomic liberation.'' August 15th marked the beginning of a new challenge: not only the liquidation of colonial rule, but also the subsequent division.
