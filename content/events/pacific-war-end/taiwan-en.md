On August 15, 1945, Emperor Showa announced the end of the war in Taiwan, which was under Japanese rule at the time. This is a historical event that marks the end of Japan's 50-year colonial rule and signifies the country's ``return'' to the Republic of China.

In Taiwanese textbooks, memories of the war's tragedies include the U.S. military's Great Air Raid on Taipei and the many Taiwanese who were conscripted into the Japanese military and sent to battlefields in the south. For this reason, Japan's surrender was the first step toward ``liberation'' from the suffering of war and mobilization, and the people of Taiwan at the time welcomed it with great anticipation and joy at the idea of ​​``returning to their homeland.''

On October 25th, an acceptance ceremony was held at Taipei Public Hall, and "Taiwan Liberation" was officially declared. However, the confusion that arose during the rapid dismantling of the Japanese governing system and the transition to the new Republic of China's rule laid the foundation for the complex historical memory of Taiwanese society thereafter. The end of the war was not just the arrival of peace, but the beginning of the formation of a new identity and the reorganization of social order.
