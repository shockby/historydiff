In 1945, President Harry S. Truman decided that a large-scale military invasion of mainland Japan (Operation Downfall) would be necessary, and the number of casualties for both the U.S. military and the Japanese side was predicted to be in the millions.

In American textbooks, the dropping of the atomic bomb is described as a "military and political decision to bring the war to an early end and save the lives of hundreds of thousands of American and even Japanese citizens." The drops on Hiroshima on August 6, 1945, and Nagasaki on August 9, 1945, were overwhelming displays of force that signaled to the Japanese leadership that immediate and unconditional surrender was the only option.

The success of the Manhattan Project is regarded as the pinnacle of science and technology at the time, and as a decisive factor in ending the long war that began with Japan's surprise attack on Pearl Harbor. Japan's declaration of surrender on August 14th (V-J Day) is portrayed as a pivotal victory in the long fight to protect democracy and freedom.
