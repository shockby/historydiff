The territorial issue between China and Russia (former Soviet Union) is a history of conflicts and treaties over the borders of the two superpowers since the 17th century. This history provides an important context for understanding the international order in East Asia for Japan as well.

### Treaty of Nerchinsk (1689)

It was the first official border treaty between the Qing Dynasty and the Russian Empire. The Russian Cossacks, who were advancing eastward into Siberia, clashed with the Qing Dynasty, which was focused on defending its northern borders, and a treaty was signed after the battle over Albazin Fortress. The Waixing'anling Mountains (Stanovoy Mountains) and the Argun River were designated as borders, and the Amur River Basin was recognized as territory of the Qing Dynasty. This is said to be the first treaty of equal equality that the Qing Dynasty concluded with a Western European nation.

### Treaty of Aygun (1858) and Treaty of Peking (1860)

In the mid-19th century, as the Qing Dynasty was weakened by the Arrow War (Second Opium War), Russia advanced into the Amur River basin under the leadership of Muravyov, the governor of Eastern Siberia. According to the Treaty of Agun, the left bank (northern bank) of the Amur River became Russian territory, and the Treaty of Beijing also ceded the coastal region east of the Ussuri River to Russia. As a result, Russia acquired Vladivostok on the Pacific Ocean. China regards these as ``unequal treaties,'' but Russia considers them to be valid international treaties.

### Sino-Soviet border conflict (1969)

In March 1969, as the Sino-Soviet conflict intensified, an armed conflict occurred between the Chinese and Soviet forces on Zhenbao Island (Damansky Island) in the Ussuri River. China said it was a defense of its own territory, and the Soviet Union said it was a planned provocation by China. This conflict posed a serious crisis, with even the possibility of nuclear war being talked about. In the same year, clashes also occurred in Jalan Ashkol, Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region.

### Final demarcation of borders

Since 1991, after the end of the Cold War, China and Russia have gradually established a border line of approximately 4,300 kilometers. In 2004, an agreement was reached on the division of the last disputed island, Bolshoi Ussuri Island, and the territorial issue between China and Russia was legally resolved. However, in terms of historical awareness, differences of opinion still exist between China and Russia regarding the nature of the 19th century treaty.
