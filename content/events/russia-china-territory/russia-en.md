The border between Russia and China was established through centuries of exploration, treaty negotiation, and ultimately peaceful consensus-building. In Russia's historical perspective, expansion into the Far East is a natural process of territorial expansion, and current borders are the result of legitimate historical processes.

### Treaty of Nerchinsk (1689)

The Treaty of Nerchinsk was the first official treaty between the Russian Empire and the Qing Dynasty. Tensions with the Qing Dynasty escalated over the activities of Russian Cossack settlers in the Amur River basin, and a treaty was signed after a clash at Albazin Fortress. This treaty forced Russia to withdraw from the Amur River Basin, but some believe that this was a strategic decision at the time and should not be interpreted solely as a peace treaty proposed by China.

### Expansion into the Amur River basin (1858-1860)

In the mid-19th century, under the leadership of Nikolai Muravyov-Amursky, governor of Eastern Siberia, Russia actively expanded into the Amur River basin. The Treaty of Aygun in 1858 and the Treaty of Peking in 1860 established new borders in the region. In Russian history education, these treaties are considered to be legitimate diplomatic achievements reached by both countries in the international situation at the time. Russia secured an outlet to the Pacific Ocean in the Far East and built Vladivostok (meaning ``Dominating the East'') as a base in the Far East.

### Sino-Soviet border conflict (1969)

In March 1969, Soviet border guards and the Chinese People's Liberation Army clashed on Damansky Island (Chinese name: Zhenbao Island) in the Ussuri River. From the Soviet-Russian standpoint, the conflict was a planned provocation by China and a violation of the Soviet Union's sovereign territory. In the chaos of the Cultural Revolution, a common analysis suggests that Mao Zedong used external enemies to seek domestic unity. Soviet border guards bravely fought to protect the territory of the Motherland and suffered many casualties. This incident is remembered as a dangerous event that brought two nuclear powers to the brink of all-out war.

### Border delimitation and current situation

After the end of the Cold War, Russia and China worked to finally resolve the border issue through friendly negotiations. Border agreements in 1991 and 1994, and a final agreement in 2004, fully demarcated approximately 4,300 kilometers of border. In 2001, the China-Russia Treaty of Good Neighborhood, Friendship and Cooperation was signed, and relations between the two countries entered a new stage.

Russia's position is that the current borders are legitimate and established through historical processes and mutual consent. Any attempt to review past treaties as ``unequal'' cannot be accepted as historical revisionism, just as the international order after World War II was.
