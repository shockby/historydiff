South Korea claims that the "East Sea (Donghae)" is the historically legitimate name for the sea area located between the Korean Peninsula and the Japanese archipelago.

According to Korean textbooks and government opinions, the name "East Sea" has a history of over 2,000 years. In addition to the Samguk Sagi (1145), which describes the founding myth of Goguryeo, the name ``East Sea'' has also been confirmed to be used in classical Chinese literature and maps from the Joseon period. According to research by the South Korean government, various names were used in maps of Europe before the 18th century, such as the ``East Sea,'' ``Sea of ​​Korea,'' and ``Oriental Sea,'' and they argue that the establishment of the single name ``Sea of ​​Japan'' was the result of Japan's military rise and imperialist expansion in the late 19th century.

South Korea believes that the adoption of the name ``Sea of ​​Japan'' at a time when it lost its right to speak in the international community due to the annexation of South Korea in 1910 is a vestige of colonial rule. Since 1992, the United Nations Conference on Standardization of Place Names and the International Hydrographic Organization (IHO) have been vigorously requesting that "Tokai" be added to the list. The IHO's decision in 2020 to transition to a new method of managing sea areas using numerical identifiers rather than names is seen by South Korea as a step toward changing the exclusive use of the single name "Sea of ​​Japan." The South Korean government is ultimately aiming to have the term ``East Sea'' listed internationally, calling this a part of restoring historical justice.
