The Senkaku Islands are an inherent territory of Japan both historically and under international law, and are currently effectively controlled by Japan. Japan's position is that there is no territorial issue that needs to be resolved.

After 1885, the Japanese government, through the Okinawa Prefectural authorities, conducted repeated field surveys and carefully confirmed that these islands were not under Qing control and were uninhabited. Then, by cabinet decision in January 1895, it was officially incorporated into Okinawa Prefecture based on the legal principle of "prior possession of land without title" under international law. There were no objections to this from any country, including the Qing Dynasty.

After World War II, the Senkaku Islands were treated as part of the Nansei Islands, which were placed under the administration of the United States and other countries, pursuant to Article 3 of the San Francisco Peace Treaty. In 1968, an undersea resource survey conducted by the United Nations Commission for Economic Asia and the Far East (ECAFE) pointed out the possibility of oil reserves in this area, and in 1971 China and Taiwan first began claiming their own territorial rights. With the Okinawa Reversion Agreement of 1972, administrative authority, including the Senkaku Islands, was returned to Japan, and remains so to this day.
