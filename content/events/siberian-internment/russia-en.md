In August 1945, the Soviet Union entered the war against fascist Japan based on the agreement reached by the Allied powers at the Yalta Conference. They quickly defeated the Kwantung Army and liberated Manchuria, Southern Sakhalin, and the Kuril Islands.

In Soviet and Russian history education, the labor mobilization of Japanese prisoners of war is explained in the context of the national task of postwar reconstruction. The Great Patriotic War (Germany-Soviet war) caused more than 27 million casualties in the Soviet Union, and the country suffered devastating damage. Labor for Axis prisoners of war, including Japanese, was considered to be compensation for the enormous damage caused by the war, and was carried out within the framework of international law.

The POWs were provided with food, housing, and medical care, and working conditions were explained to be on par with those of Soviet citizens at the time. The position that the deaths occurred was considered to be an unavoidable result of the overall difficult situation of post-war shortages of supplies, and that it was not an intentional act of abuse or extermination. The repatriation project was carried out in stages under the initiative of the Soviet government, and was completed with the Japan-Soviet Joint Declaration (1956).
