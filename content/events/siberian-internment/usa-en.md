In August 1945, the Soviet Union entered the war against Japan under the Yalta Agreement. After Japan surrendered, the Soviet Union deported large numbers of Japanese prisoners of war to Siberia and other regions where they were forced to work.

In American textbooks and historical materials, Siberian internment is primarily mentioned in the context of the origins of the Cold War and the East-West conflict. The Soviet Union's long-term internment of Japanese prisoners of war goes against the principle of ``prompt return of Japanese troops'' as stipulated in the Potsdam Declaration, and is sometimes seen as an example of the Soviet Union's pattern of behavior in the post-war international order, namely its non-compliance with agreements and prioritizing its own interests.

There is also a critical viewpoint that the ``Yalta Secret Agreement,'' in which President Roosevelt accepted the attachment of Southern Sakhalin and the Kuril Islands to the Soviet Union at the Yalta Conference in exchange for the Soviet Union's participation in the war against Japan, was a contributing factor to the Soviet Union's postwar territorial expansion and internment of Japanese people. Since the United States itself had a similar problem with the internment of Japanese Americans, some textbooks sometimes discuss the two in a comparative context.
