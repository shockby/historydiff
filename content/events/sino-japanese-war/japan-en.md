In 1894, the Donghak Party Rebellion (Kogo Peasants' War) on the Korean Peninsula led to a conflict between Japan and Qing over Korea that escalated into an armed conflict. This is the Sino-Japanese War.

In Japanese textbooks, the Sino-Japanese War is positioned as a war that tested the results of Japan's modernization after the Meiji Restoration. The victory of Japan, equipped with modern military power, over the old-fashioned Qing Dynasty was an epoch-making event that completely changed the balance of power in Asia. It describes military successes such as the combined fleet's victory in the Battle of the Yellow Sea and the capture of Weihaiwei.

In 1895, the Treaty of Shimonoseki (Baseki Treaty) recognized the independence of Korea, ceded the Liaodong Peninsula, Taiwan, and the Penghu Islands to Japan, and stipulated that it would pay compensation of 200 million taels. However, due to the triple intervention by Russia, France, and Germany, Japan was forced to return the Liaodong Peninsula to Qing China. This humiliation spread among the people as a spirit of ``gagaki kagar'' and became a foreshadowing of the later Russo-Japanese War.
