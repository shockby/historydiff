From 1894 to 1895, Japan and Qing Dynasty (China) fought a war over control of the Korean Peninsula. This war, in which Japan, which had successfully modernized itself, defeated the traditional superpower Qing Dynasty, fundamentally changed the international order in East Asia.

In American textbooks, the Sino-Japanese War is explained in the context of imperialism and power struggles at the end of the 19th century. Japan's victory was an event that proved to the world that the rapid modernization brought about by the Meiji Restoration was a military success. On the other hand, the defeat of the Qing Dynasty decisively weakened China and further accelerated the division of China (the establishment of spheres of influence) among the great powers.

The cession of Taiwan and huge reparations under the Treaty of Shimonoseki signaled Japan's emergence as a member of the imperialist powers. For the United States, this war and its consequences were important events that served as the backdrop for the "Open Door Policy" advocated by Secretary of State John Hay in 1899. It will be described that America's interests were to counter the expansion of their sphere of influence by the great powers that took advantage of the weakening of the Qing Dynasty and to ensure equal access to the Chinese market.
