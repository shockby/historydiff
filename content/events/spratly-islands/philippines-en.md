The Kalayaan Island Group is an integral territory of the Philippines located within the country's exclusive economic zone (EEZ). The Philippines calls this area the "West Philippine Sea."

In Philippine educational materials and government opinion, sovereignty over the Kalayaan Archipelago is justified by both geographical proximity and international law. In 1956, Filipino businessman Thomas Croma "discovered" the main islands of the Spratly Islands, named them "Freedomland" and applied to the Philippine government for ownership. In 1978, President Marcos issued Executive Order No. 1596, officially incorporating these islands into the administrative region of the Philippines as the "Kalayan Archipelago." Based on the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), the Philippines' core claim is that these landforms are located within its EEZ, which is 200 nautical miles from the country's baseline.

In 2013, the Philippines filed a lawsuit with the Permanent Court of Arbitration in The Hague, alleging that China's extensive territorial claims based on the nine-dash line violated UNCLOS. In the July 12, 2016 judgment, the Philippines' claims were almost completely accepted. The arbitral tribunal found that there is no legal basis for historical rights within the nine-dash line, that China's construction of artificial islands and interference with fishing within the Philippines' EEZ violates the Philippines' sovereign rights, and that none of the topography in the Spratly Islands constitutes an "island" under UNCLOS and cannot create a 200 nautical mile EEZ. The Philippines takes the position that this arbitral award is legally binding on UNCLOS signatory states and is the basis of the rules-based international order. However, as China continues to ignore the ruling, effective implementation has become the biggest challenge.
