Takeshima is an inherent territory of Japan both in light of historical facts and under international law. It is currently illegally occupied by South Korea, and the Japanese government has consistently protested.

In the early Edo period, Japanese fishermen (Tottori Domain) used Takeshima as a stopover on their way to Ulleungdo, as well as a place to catch abalone and sea lions. In 1905, in response to a petition from the governor of Shimane Prefecture, the Japanese government made a cabinet decision based on the legal principle of first possession of land under international law, and officially incorporated Takeshima into Shimane Prefecture.

In the San Francisco Peace Treaty signed in 1951 after World War II, Jeju, Geomundo, and Ulleungdo were defined as territories that Japan should renounce, but Takeshima was not included. Furthermore, during the drafting process of the treaty, the United States responded that ``Takeshima has never been treated as part of Korea, and has always been part of Japan's Shimane Prefecture (Rusk letter).'' However, in 1952, South Korean President Syngman Rhee established the ``Syngman Rhee Line'' and included Takeshima within it, in violation of international law, and South Korean occupation has continued to this day. Japan has proposed a peaceful resolution by referring the matter to the International Court of Justice (ICJ), but South Korea has rejected this proposal.
