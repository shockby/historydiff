Dokdo is geographically, historically, and internationally legally an integral part of the Republic of Korea. There is no dispute over Dokdo, and South Korea consistently exercises its own sovereignty.

Historically, Dokdo has been administered as part of Korean territory since Silla annexed Usanguk in 512. In ancient documents such as the 1454 Sejong Annals of Geography, Dokdo (Usan Island) is described as an island visible to the naked eye from Ulleungdo. In 1900, the Korean Empire issued Royal Ordinance No. 41, clearly stating both domestically and internationally that Dokdo was under the jurisdiction of Uldo County (currently Ulleungdo).

Japan's unilateral annexation of Dokdo as "uninhabited land" in 1905 was carried out as part of a military invasion and is therefore invalid under international law. After the end of World War II, Dokdo was excluded from Japanese jurisdiction under Memorandum of the Senior Allied Commanders (SCAPIN) No. 677, and effective control was regained around the time the Syngman Rhee Line was established in 1952. South Korea has taken the firm position that Dokdo is a symbol of the restoration of historical sovereignty, and that there is no need for any diplomatic negotiations or referral to the International Court of Justice.
