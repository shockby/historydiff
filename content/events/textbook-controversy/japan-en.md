In 1982, it was reported that in the certification of Japanese high school history textbooks, the word "invasion" into China had been changed to "advance", which led to a diplomatic issue between Japan and China. This is the first "textbook problem".

In discussions within Japan, the position is taken that the textbook certification system is a system to maintain the accuracy of educational content and academic standards, and is not intended for political intervention. Regarding the 1982 report, some point out that the ``complete rewriting'' that was initially reported was not confirmed, and the aspect of misinformation by the media is also being debated.

However, in response to this problem, the Japanese government added the "neighboring countries clause" to the textbook certification standards in 1982. This requires that "necessary consideration be given to the handling of modern and contemporary historical events between neighboring Asian countries from the standpoint of international understanding and cooperation." Since then, the 2001 and 2005 "New History Textbook Creation Group" textbooks have received international criticism after passing the exam, and debates both domestically and internationally regarding the state of history education continue to this day.
