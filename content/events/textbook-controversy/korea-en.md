Since 1982, the Japanese government, through its textbook certification system, has rewritten "invasion" into the Korean peninsula as "expansion" and glorified the reality of colonial rule. This is a "distortion of history" that cannot be overlooked by South Korea.

In history education and public opinion in South Korea, the Japanese textbook issue has an extremely heavy meaning as a symbol of the unfinished liquidation of colonial rule. The following points are particularly criticized as serious problems. First, it describes the annexation of Korea in 1910 as a ``legitimate treaty'' and glorifies colonial rule as ``contributing to modernization.'' The second is the deletion or reduction of descriptions of Japanese military "comfort women," which is seen as an act that tramples on the dignity of the victims. Third, it describes Dokdo (Takeshima) as ``inherent territory of Japan'' and denies South Korea's sovereignty.

Textbooks from the Society for Creating New History Textbooks, which passed certification in 2001 and 2005, sparked violent protests and diplomatic confrontations within South Korea. The South Korean government has repeatedly called on Japan to demonstrate "deep remorse for its past colonial rule and invasion." The textbook issue, along with the Yasukuni Shrine visit, the comfort women issue, and the Dokdo issue, is the most deeply-rooted issue in Japan-Korea relations, and there is a widely shared position that unless historic reconciliation is achieved, it will be difficult to build a truly future-oriented relationship.
