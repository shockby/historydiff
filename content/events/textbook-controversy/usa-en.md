Since 1982, the treatment of war descriptions in Japanese history textbooks has repeatedly led to diplomatic issues with China and South Korea.

In American academic circles and diplomatic analysis, Japan's textbook issue is studied as a typical example of the ``politicization of history'' in East Asia. While the war descriptions in Japanese textbooks are criticized as ``revisionist'' by neighboring countries, textbooks from China and South Korea also contain elements of ``patriotic education'' that are biased toward their own countries' perspectives, and there is analysis that the problem is not one-sided.

What is particularly noteworthy from an American perspective is that the textbook issue is discussed in comparison with Germany. After the war, Germany acknowledged full responsibility for the Holocaust and even went so far as to create joint textbooks with neighboring countries (such as France). In contrast, Japan has published government opinions such as the ``Murayama Statement'' and ``Kono Statement,'' but the content of textbooks is left to the discretion of publishers and the certification system, and no unified approach has been taken. For American diplomatic authorities, the textbook issue is of concern as a ``historical friction'' that is worsening relations between Japan and South Korea, allies of the United States and the United States, and is also being watched closely as one of the factors hindering security cooperation in East Asia.
