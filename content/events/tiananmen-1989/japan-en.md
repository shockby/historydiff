In April 1989, the death of Hu Yaobang, former general secretary of the Communist Party of China, who was known as a reformist, sparked a large-scale democratization movement led by university students in Beijing. Students gathered in Tiananmen Square to demand political reform, freedom of speech, and an end to bureaucratic corruption. The movement expanded to include intellectuals, workers, and citizens, and spread not only to Beijing but also to Shanghai, Guangzhou, and other parts of the country. At its peak, more than 1 million people are said to have gathered in Tiananmen Square.

On May 20th, the Chinese government declared martial law in Beijing. General Secretary Zhao Ziyang was ousted for showing sympathy for students, and hard-line Prime Minister Li Peng and others took control. From the night of June 3rd to the early hours of June 4th, the Chinese People's Liberation Army controlled the area around Tiananmen Square with tanks and armed troops. The military opened fire on civilians on Chang'an Street and other roads leading to the square, resulting in many casualties.

The Chinese government officially announced that the number of victims was 319, but the prevailing view internationally is that the actual number was significantly higher. After the incident, the Chinese government arrested a large number of those involved, and many democratic activists went into exile overseas. The Tiananmen Incident was a tragedy that occurred during the global trend of democratization at the end of the Cold War.In contrast to the revolutions in Eastern Europe that led to the collapse of communist systems, in China the Communist Party's one-party rule was maintained by force. The incident is still subject to strict censorship in China, and it is taboo to discuss it in public.
