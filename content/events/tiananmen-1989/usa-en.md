In the spring of 1989, millions of students, workers, and citizens rose up in Beijing, the capital of China, demanding democracy and freedom. Tiananmen Square became a center of peaceful protests and a Statue of Democracy statue, modeled after the Statue of Liberty, was erected. This movement was a symbolic event at the end of the Cold War, when there were a series of popular uprisings against communist dictatorships around the world.

But on June 4, 1989, the Chinese Communist Party made the shocking decision to turn tanks and troops against its own citizens, with the world watching. The People's Liberation Army brutally attacked unarmed protesters in and around Tiananmen Square, killing hundreds to thousands. The iconic image of a man standing in front of a convoy of tanks (“Tankman”) has become a global icon of individual courage against tyranny.

The Tiananmen Massacre was widely condemned by the international community, and Western countries, including the United States, imposed arms embargoes and economic sanctions on China. However, the Chinese Communist Party does not accept any responsibility and is thoroughly censoring and deleting information about the incident, attempting to erase it from the public's memory. The Tiananmen Incident was one of the greatest human rights violations of the 20th century, violently trampling on the universal values ​​of democracy and human rights, and demonstrated to the world the essential brutality of authoritarian regimes. The United States commemorates the Tiananmen Square massacre every June 4 and continues to advocate for political freedom and human rights in China.
