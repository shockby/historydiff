During World War II, the Japanese military secretly operated a large-scale biological weapons research program in Harbin, northeastern China (Manchuria). At the center of this is an organization called Unit 731.

In American history textbooks and academic materials, Unit 731 is referred to as a "war crime comparable to Nazi human experimentation." It is recorded that under the command of Lieutenant General Shiro Ishii, human experiments using plague, cholera, anthrax, etc. were carried out on thousands of prisoners of war, as well as frostbite experiments and vivisections.

Particularly important in the description of the United States is the part about **postwar immunity trading**. After Japan's defeat in the war in 1945, American military intelligence determined that Unit 731's research data would be extremely useful for biological weapons defense during the Cold War. As a result, a deal was made in which key members of Unit 731, including Shiro Ishii, were given immunity from prosecution for war crimes in exchange for providing experimental data. As a result, no one associated with Unit 731 was indicted at the Tokyo Trials (International Military Tribunal for the Far East). This American decision has been critically discussed as a classic example of Cold War realpolitik and as an ethical dilemma between justice and national security.
